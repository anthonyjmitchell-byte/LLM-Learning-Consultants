Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Analysis of Histograms

The histograms with kernel density overlays reveal how Computer Science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and Benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating widespread adoption and perceived productivity gains. In contrast, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a critical gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, yet they report limited deep learning outcomes.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Analysis and Interpretation of the Boxplot

This boxplot provides a clear statistical summary of central tendency, spread, and outliers. Usage Frequency and Perceived Risks show higher medians, while Perceived Learning Impact has the lowest median and widest variability.

Meaning and Interpretation: The boxplot shows central tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, while Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, confirming weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage (mean ~2.1/3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains (mean ~1.7/3.25)
  • Relatively high Perceived Benefits
  • Moderately high Risk Level
  • Low average Problematic Behavior

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Detailed Interpretation

The radar chart for current Computer Science students reveals a distinct profile characterized by prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency. These high scores indicate that today’s undergraduates have deeply integrated Large Language Models into their daily academic routines. In contrast, the Learning Impact spoke is noticeably shorter, reflecting limited perceived gains in deep conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention.

2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Detailed Interpretation

The alumni radar chart shows a more balanced but still concerning profile. Alumni report noticeably lower Dependency and Usage Frequency, while the Learning Impact spoke remains short and weak — similar to current students.

3. LLM Impact Comparison: Students vs Alumni

Figure 11: LLM Impact Comparison – Students vs Alumni

Detailed Interpretation

Current students score substantially higher on Usage Frequency and LLM Dependency. Both groups display very low scores on Learning Impact. This exposes a systemic gap between rapid LLM adoption and meaningful improvements in core Computer Science competencies, strongly supporting the capstone’s central thesis.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations, and Documentation

Modeling Assumptions and Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to Lewis University which reduces generalizability
  • Possible response and recall bias — especially for alumni
