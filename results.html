Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current computer science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores: Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk

Analysis of Histograms

The histograms with density overlays show how computer science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating adoption and perceived productivity gains. However, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, they report limited deep learning outcomes. Dependency is bimodal, leaning towards two user groups: light and heavy users. Risk perception is spread out and behavioral change is low.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and risk are skewed toward higher values, Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived learning gains.

Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Analysis and Interpretation of the Boxplot

This plot provides a summary of median tendency, spread, and outliers for each of the six composite scores. Usage frequency and perceived risks show higher medians, perceived learning impact has the lowest median and widest variability. This confirms the weak translation from heavy LLM usage to deep learning outcomes in education.

Meaning and Interpretation: The boxplot shows tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, which means weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Findings from Composite Scores:

Individual Score Distributions

Figure 3: Distribution of LLM Usage Scores (Students)

Analysis – Usage Frequency Mean = 1.97

The distribution is right-skewed with a peak between 1.5 and 2.0, and another rise at 3.0. The red dashed line marks the mean at 1.97. This indicates strong adoption of LLMs among computer science students for daily academic tasks such as coding, debugging, research, and assignment completion. A significant portion of students use LLMs at a moderate intensity, which means that these tools have become a part of the modern CS workflow.

Meaning: Strong adoption of LLMs in daily academic tasks.

Figure 4: Distribution of LLM Dependency Scores for Students

Analysis – LLM Dependency Mean = 2.02

This distribution shows a peak at 2.0 and a rise at 3.0, with the mean at 2.02. The shape indicates moderate reliance on LLMs for core academic tasks. Some students maintain lower dependency scores below 1.5, but a large group has integrated LLMs into their problem-solving process. This tendency points to two distinct user types: those who use LLMs occasionally as a supplement and those who use them as a primary assistance tool.

Meaning: Moderate-to-high reliance on LLMs for core tasks.

Figure 5: Distribution of Perceived Learning Impact Scores (Students)

Analysis – Perceived Learning Impact Mean = 1.60

The distribution is centered around 1.6–1.8 with a mean of 1.60. Although a smaller group reports higher learning gains, the majority of responses show around moderate-to-low scores. This pattern reveals a gap: even with high usage and dependency, students perceive only limited improvements in conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention of knowledge. The curve emphasizes that learning gains are behind adoption rates.

Meaning and Analysis: Shifted toward lower scores, showing limited perceived gains in conceptual understanding and critical thinking despite high usage.

Figure 6: Distribution of Perceived Benefit Scores - Students

Analysis – Perceived Benefits Mean = 1.12

This distribution is right-skewed, with the highest bar at the maximum score 1.4–1.5 and a mean of 1.12. Students report benefits from LLM use in productivity, in speed of task completion, and assistance with coding/debugging work. The shape indicates these benefits are surface-level - not good for deeper learning.

Meaning: Clear productivity benefits, but mostly surface-level.

Figure 7: Distribution of Perceived Risk Scores (Students)

Analysis – Perceived Risks (Mean = 2.48)

The distribution is roughly bell-shaped and peaks around 2.5–3.0 and a mean of 2.48. A portion of students also scores at the higher end 4–5. This indicates moderately high awareness of the risks associated with LLM use, including potential skill atrophy, over-dependence, ethical concerns, and accuracy issues. The spread shows varied levels of risk perception across the student population.

Meaning: Reasonable awareness of risks associated with LLM use.

Figure 8: Distribution of Problematic Behavior Scores (Students)

Analysis – Behavioral Change Mean = 0.45

This distribution is skewed toward low scores, with a mean of only 0.45. Most students report minimal problematic behavioral changes. A tail extends to higher scores 0.8–1.0, which means that a small group of students may be experiencing shifts to reduced independent problem-solving or over-reliance on AI-generated solutions. Overall, this behavior is limited.

Meaning: Generally low problematic behaviors, though some higher scores exist.

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates. Normalization allows visual and statistical comparison across groups even with any differences in how the original scales were constructed.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Interpretation

The radar chart for current computer science students shows a profile characterized by spikes in usage and dependency. These scores indicate that undergraduates have integrated Large Language Models into their academic routines — for code generation, debugging, and generating ideas for assignment support.

The learning impact spoke is shorter, which means limited perceived gains in conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention. Scores in perceived benefits indicate students value the productivity advantages LLMs provide, but risk and behavior remain at moderate levels.

This pattern highights the goal of our project of mapping current integration patterns and highlights an inportant part: heavy adoption and short-term productivity gains have not lead to stronger deep learning outcomes. This highlights the need for strategies that have more mindful, active, and better supported use of LLMs rather than passive reliance on it.

Meaning and Interpretation: Current CS students integrate LLMs but see limited gains in deep learning skills. Moderate benefit and moderate risk/behavior means there are productivity advantages.

2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Interpretation

The alumni radar chart shows a more balanced but an intersting profile. Compared to current students, alumni report lower dependency and usage frequency. This reduction can be a because of changes in professional workflows, greater experience with traditional problem-solving methods, or reduced academic pressure after their graduation.

The learning impact is small — similar to that of current students. The low perceived learning gains indicate that the usage–learning gap in undergraduate studies does not resolve itself after graduation. Moderate levels of perceived benefits, risk, and behavior are comparable between the students and alumni groups. This indicates that the advantages and risk awareness of LLMs carry on into career life and that deep learning outcomes are behind.

These findings show that LLM reliance during university years may not build stronger long-term skills unless intentionally supported by instructional design. The alumni profile is an indicator that without intervention, current students risk entering their future careers with productivity tools but weak independent problem-solving and conceptual understanding.

Meaning and Interpretation: Alumni show lower dependency and usage than current students. Learning impact remains weak, meaning that the usage–learning gap may persist after graduation. Benefit and risk/behavior levels are comparable. This means that LLM reliance during studies does not automatically result in stronger long-term learning outcomes.

3. LLM Impact Comparison: Students vs Alumni

Figure 11: LLM Impact Comparison – Students vs Alumni

Interpretation

The side-by-side bar comparison provides a visualization of differences and similarities between the two groups. Current students score higher on usage frequency and LLM dependency. This confirms more integration of generative AI tools during their studies.

Both groups show average scores in prceived benefits, meaning that LLMs are valued for efficiency, but their perceived value is not at the transformative level. Both current students and alumni display low scores on learning impact — the shortest bar in the comparison. This weakness across both groups shows a gap between LLM adoption and meaningful improvements in computer science competencies such as algorithmic thinking, system design, debugging, and understanding.

The comparable levels of risk and behavior indicate awareness of potential downsides exists but this has not led to behavioral shifts or better learning outcomes. Overall, this analysis supports the our research's main thesis: LLM usage delivers short-term productivity gains but fails to develop deep, long term learning. The persistence of this gap from student years into alumni experience calls for curriculum-level changes, including redesigned assessments that do not need over-reliance on LLMs, and assignments that require students to explain in class and discuss about AI-generated outputs.

Meaning and Interpretation: Students score higher on Usage and Dependency. Both groups show benefits and low learning impact. This shows a gap between LLM adoption and improvements in CS competencies.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations, and Documentation

Modeling Assumptions and Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses reflect participants’ experiences
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs of the research
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without distortion
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights
  • LLM usage patterns were stable during data collection

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one Lewis University which reduces generalizability
  • Possible response and recall bias - especially for alumni

Model Validation: Outputs were checked against the data, distributions checked for anomalies, and all graphs regenerated from the same dataset
