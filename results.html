Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Detailed Analysis of Histograms (Figure 1)

The histograms with kernel density overlays reveal how Computer Science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and Benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating widespread adoption and perceived productivity gains. In contrast, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a critical gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, yet they report limited deep learning outcomes. Dependency is bimodal, suggesting two distinct groups — light and heavy users. Risk perception is moderately spread, while Behavioral Change remains mostly low.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Detailed Analysis and Interpretation of the Boxplot (Figure 2)

This boxplot provides a clear statistical summary of central tendency (median), spread (interquartile range), and outliers for each of the six composite scores. The y-axis represents the composite score values (note that the scales differ slightly per dimension based on how the composites were constructed, but comparisons within the plot remain valid).

Key Observations by Dimension:

  • LLM Dependency (light blue): Median around 2.0–2.2, with a wide interquartile range (IQR) and whiskers extending from ~0.3 to ~3.2. This shows moderate-to-high dependency with significant variability — some students barely rely on LLMs, while others depend on them heavily for coursework and problem-solving.
  • Perceived Learning Impact (light blue): Lowest median of all dimensions (approximately 1.7). The box is relatively narrow but has the widest overall spread and several high outliers. This confirms that while a few students report strong learning gains, the typical student experiences only modest benefits in terms of deep conceptual understanding and skill retention.
  • Behavioral Change (light blue): Very low median (~0.5) and tight clustering near the bottom. Most students show minimal negative behavioral shifts (e.g., reduced critical thinking or over-reliance leading to academic shortcuts). The presence of a few higher outliers suggests that problematic behavior is not widespread but exists in a small subset of students.
  • Perceived Benefits (medium blue): Median around 1.3, with a compact IQR. Students consistently report strong practical benefits such as faster coding, better debugging, and improved productivity. The distribution is positively skewed, reinforcing that LLMs are viewed as highly useful tools in the short term.
  • Usage Frequency (dark blue): High median (~2.0–2.5) and a solid IQR from ~1.5 to ~2.8. This dimension has one of the highest central tendencies, confirming frequent and regular use of LLMs among current CS students.
  • Perceived Risks (dark blue): Highest median of the set (approximately 2.5–2.8), with a relatively wide box and upper whisker reaching 3.3+. Students demonstrate substantial awareness of potential downsides, including skill atrophy, ethical issues, dependency risks, and accuracy concerns. The distribution shows meaningful variability in risk perception.

Central Thesis Reinforced: The boxplot vividly illustrates the disconnect between high Usage Frequency and Perceived Benefits on one side, and the relatively low Perceived Learning Impact on the other. Despite heavy adoption and recognized advantages, the median learning outcome remains the weakest among all dimensions. This “usage–learning gap” is the most important finding of the study and strongly supports the need for pedagogical interventions that promote more mindful and effective LLM integration in Computer Science education.

Additionally, the higher median for Perceived Risks compared to Behavioral Change suggests that students are cognitively aware of the dangers but have not yet widely translated that awareness into changed behavior.

Meaning and Interpretation (Summary): The boxplot shows central tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, while Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, confirming weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Key Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage (mean ~2.1/3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains (mean ~1.7/3.25)
  • Relatively high Perceived Benefits
  • Moderately high Risk Level
  • Low average Problematic Behavior

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Meaning and Interpretation: This radar chart shows prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency with a shorter Learning Impact spoke. Current CS students heavily integrate LLMs but see limited gains in deep learning skills. Moderate Benefit and low-to-moderate Risk/Behavior indicate productivity advantages with reasonable caution. 2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations & Documentation

Modeling Assumptions & Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences (self-reported data).
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs.
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without major distortion.
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights.
  • LLM usage patterns were relatively stable during data collection.

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one institution (reduced generalizability).
  • Possible response and recall bias (especially for alumni).
  • Cross-sectional design (no longitudinal tracking).
  • No statistical significance testing performed due to sample size.

Model Validation: Outputs were cross-checked against raw data, distributions inspected for anomalies, and all graphs regenerated from the same dataset for reproducibility.
