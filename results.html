Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current computer ccience students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores: Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk

Analysis of Histograms

The histograms with kernel density overlays reveal how computer science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating adoption and perceived productivity gains. However, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, they report limited deep learning outcomes. Dependency is bimodal, leaning towards two user groups: light and heavy users. Risk perception is moderately spread, while Behavioral Change is mostly low.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Individual Score Distributions

Figure 3: Distribution of LLM Usage Scores (Students)

Analysis – Usage Frequency (Mean = 1.97)

The distribution is right-skewed with a clear peak between 1.5 and 2.0, followed by another rise at the highest bin 3.0. The red dashed line marks the mean at 1.97. This indicates strong and frequent adoption of LLMs among computer science students for daily academic tasks such as coding, debugging, research, and assignment completion. Figure 4: Distribution of LLM Dependency Scores for Students

Analysis – LLM Dependency (Mean = 2.02)

This distribution shows a peak at 2.0 and a secondary rise at 3.0, with the mean at 2.02. The shape indicates moderate-to-high reliance on LLMs for core academic tasks. While some students maintain lower dependency scores below 1.5, a large group has integrated LLMs into their problem-solving process. This tendency points to two distinct user types: those who use LLMs occasionally as a supplement and those who treat them as a primary assistance tool.

Meaning: Moderate-to-high reliance on LLMs for core tasks.

Figure 5: Distribution of Perceived Learning Impact Scores (Students)

Analysis – Perceived Learning Impact (Mean = 1.60)

The distribution is centered around 1.6–1.8 with a mean of 1.60. Although a smaller group reports higher learning gains (around 2.0–2.2), the majority of responses cluster at moderate-to-low scores. This pattern reveals a critical gap: despite high usage and dependency, students perceive only limited improvements in deep conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention of Computer Science knowledge. The density curve further emphasizes that true learning gains lag behind adoption rates.

Meaning and Analysis: Shifted toward lower scores, showing limited perceived gains in conceptual understanding and critical thinking despite high usage.

Figure 6: Distribution of Perceived Benefit Scores - Students

Analysis – Perceived Benefits Mean = 1.12

This distribution is right-skewed, with the highest bar at the maximum score 1.4–1.5 and a mean of 1.12. Students report strong practical benefits from LLM use, espically in productivity, in speed of task completion, and assistance with routine coding/debugging work. However, the shape indicates these benefits are mostly surface-level - rather than transformative for deeper learning.

Figure 7: Distribution of Perceived Risk Scores (Students)

Analysis – Perceived Risks (Mean = 2.48)

The distribution is roughly bell-shaped with a peak around 2.5–3.0 and a mean of 2.48. A portion of students also scores at the higher end 4–5). This indicates reasonable to moderately high awareness of the risks associated with LLM use, including potential skill atrophy, over-dependence, ethical concerns, and accuracy issues. Figure 8: Distribution of Problematic Behavior Scores (Students)

This distribution is heavily skewed toward low scores, with a mean of only 0.45. Most students report minimal problematic behavioral changes. Overall, extreme negative behaviors remain limited.

Meaning: Generally low problematic behaviors, though some higher scores exist.

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates. Normalization allows direct visual and statistical comparison across groups despite potential differences in how the original scales were constructed.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Interpretation

The radar chart for current Computer Science students reveals a distinct profile characterized by prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency. These high scores indicate that today’s undergraduates have deeply integrated Large Language Models into their daily academic routines — from code generation and debugging to idea generation and assignment support.

In contrast, the Learning Impact spoke is noticeably shorter, reflecting limited perceived gains in deep conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention of core CS competencies. Moderate scores in Perceived Benefits suggest students clearly value the productivity advantages LLMs provide, while Risk and Behavior remain at low-to-moderate levels, indicating reasonable awareness of potential downsides without widespread problematic behavioral changes.

This pattern fulfills the project’s goal of mapping current integration patterns and underscores a key tension: heavy adoption and short-term productivity gains have not yet translated into stronger deep learning outcomes. The profile highlights the urgent need for pedagogical strategies that encourage more mindful, reflective, and scaffolded use of LLMs rather than passive reliance.

Meaning and Interpretation: Current CS students heavily integrate LLMs but see limited gains in deep learning skills. Moderate Benefit and low-to-moderate Risk/Behavior indicate productivity advantages with reasonable caution. This highlights the need for better pedagogical strategies.

2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Interpretation

The alumni radar chart shows a more balanced but still concerning profile. Compared to current students, alumni report noticeably lower Dependency and Usage Frequency. This reduction may reflect changes in professional workflows, greater experience with traditional problem-solving methods, or reduced academic pressure after graduation.

Importantly, the Learning Impact spoke remains short and weak — similar to that of current students. This persistence of low perceived learning gains suggests that the usage–learning gap observed during undergraduate studies does not automatically resolve itself after graduation. Moderate levels of Perceived Benefits, Risk, and Behavior are largely comparable between the two groups, indicating that the practical advantages and risk awareness of LLMs carry forward into professional life, but deep learning outcomes lag behind.

These findings imply that heavy LLM reliance during university years may not build stronger long-term foundational skills unless deliberately supported by targeted instructional design. The alumni profile serves as a cautionary signal: without intervention, the current generation of students risks entering the workforce with solid productivity tools but weaker independent problem-solving and conceptual mastery.

Meaning and Interpretation: Alumni show lower Dependency and Usage than current students. Learning Impact remains weak, suggesting the usage–learning gap may persist after graduation. Benefit and Risk/Behavior levels are comparable. This indicates that heavy LLM reliance during studies does not automatically result in stronger long-term learning outcomes.

3. LLM Impact Comparison: Students vs Alumni

Figure 11: LLM Impact Comparison – Students vs Alumni

Interpretation

The side-by-side bar comparison provides a direct and powerful visualization of differences and similarities between the two groups. Current students score substantially higher on Usage Frequency and LLM Dependency, confirming more intensive integration of generative AI tools during their studies.

Both groups exhibit only modest scores in Perceived Benefits, suggesting that while LLMs are valued for efficiency, their perceived value does not reach transformative levels. Most strikingly, both current students and alumni display very low scores on Learning Impact — the shortest bar in the comparison. This consistent weakness across cohorts strongly exposes a systemic gap between rapid LLM adoption and meaningful improvements in core Computer Science competencies such as algorithmic thinking, system design, debugging mindset, and theoretical understanding.

The comparable levels of Risk and Behavior further suggest that awareness of potential downsides exists but has not yet led to significant behavioral shifts or stronger learning outcomes. Overall, this comparative analysis robustly supports the capstone’s central thesis: widespread LLM adoption is delivering short-term productivity gains but failing to proportionally enhance deep, durable learning. The persistence of this gap from student years into alumni experience calls for curriculum-level reforms, including explicit LLM literacy training, redesigned assessments that discourage over-reliance, and assignments that require students to explain, critique, and build upon AI-generated outputs.

Meaning and Interpretation: Students score higher on Usage and Dependency. Both groups show modest Benefit and strikingly low Learning Impact. This exposes a systemic gap between LLM adoption and improvements in core CS competencies, strongly supporting the capstone’s central thesis.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations, and Documentation

Modeling Assumptions and Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without major distortion
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights
  • LLM usage patterns were relatively stable during data collection

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one Lewis University which reduces generalizability
  • Possible response and recall bias - especially for alumni

Model Validation: Outputs were checked against the data, distributions checked for anomalies, and all graphs regenerated from the same dataset
