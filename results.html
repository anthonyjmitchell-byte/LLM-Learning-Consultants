Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk) for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. The Usage and Risk dimensions show distributions skewed toward higher values, indicating frequent LLM adoption and notable awareness of potential risks. In contrast, the Learning dimension peaks at lower scores, revealing that despite widespread use, students perceive only moderate improvements in conceptual understanding, problem-solving, and critical thinking skills. This visualization fulfills the project goal of quantifying the gap between LLM usage intensity and actual learning outcomes. It also highlights the need for educators to implement structured guidance when integrating LLMs into coursework to convert high usage into measurable learning gains.

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Meaning and Interpretation: The boxplot provides a clear side-by-side view of central tendency, spread, and outliers for each composite score. Usage and Risk exhibit higher medians and tighter distributions, while Learning shows the lowest median and widest variability. This confirms that LLM integration is strongest in day-to-day task support but weakest in fostering deep learning. The visualization validates the survey-based modeling approach and underscores key limitations such as self-reported data bias and the cross-sectional nature of the study.

Key Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage: Students heavily use LLMs (Usage_Student mean ~ 2.1 / 3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains: Learning_Student mean ~ 1.7 / 3.25
  • Perceived Benefits: Relatively high (Benefit_Student mean ~ 1.25 / 1.75)
  • Risk Level: Moderately high (Risk_Student mean ~ 2.5 / 3.0)
  • Problematic behavior: Remains relatively low on average (Behavior_Student mean ~ 0.45 / 1.0)

Individual Score Distributions

These histograms break down each dimension individually for current students, allowing detailed examination of response patterns.

Figure 3: Distribution of LLM Usage Scores (Students)

Meaning: This histogram shows strong adoption of LLMs among current CS students, with most responses clustered in the higher usage categories. It demonstrates that LLMs have become a routine part of the student workflow for coding, debugging, and research tasks.

Figure 4: Distribution of LLM Dependency Scores (Students)

Meaning: Students exhibit moderate-to-high dependency on LLMs, suggesting reliance not only for convenience but also for core academic tasks. This raises important questions about skill retention when LLM assistance is removed.

Figure 5: Distribution of Perceived Learning Impact Scores (Students)

Meaning and Interpretation: The distribution is noticeably shifted toward lower scores, indicating that frequent LLM use has not translated into strong perceived gains in deep learning, retention, or critical thinking. This is one of the most significant findings of the study and directly addresses the central research question regarding the true educational value of LLMs in CS education.

Figure 6: Distribution of Perceived Benefit Scores (Students)

Meaning: Students generally perceive clear benefits from LLM use, particularly in productivity and efficiency. However, these benefits appear more surface-level than transformative for long-term skill development.

Figure 7: Distribution of Perceived Risk Scores (Students)

Meaning: Students demonstrate reasonable awareness of risks such as over-reliance, reduced originality, and potential inaccuracies. This balanced view suggests growing digital literacy regarding generative AI tools.

Figure 8: Distribution of Problematic Behavior Scores (Students)

Meaning: Problematic behaviors (e.g., submitting LLM-generated work without understanding) remain relatively low on average, which is an encouraging sign. However, the presence of some higher scores indicates that targeted interventions may still be necessary.

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Meaning and Interpretation: This radar chart visualizes the average normalized scores across six dimensions for current undergraduate Computer Science students at Lewis University. The prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency, combined with a noticeably shorter spoke in Learning Impact, reveal a clear pattern: students are heavily integrating LLMs into their academic routines, yet this integration is not strongly enhancing deeper learning outcomes such as conceptual mastery or critical thinking. The moderate Benefit score alongside low-to-moderate Risk and Behavior scores suggests students are gaining productivity advantages while maintaining reasonable caution. This profile fulfills the project objective of mapping LLM integration patterns and highlights the urgent need for pedagogical strategies that bridge the usage–learning gap.

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Compared to current students, alumni show lower Dependency and Usage, likely due to less academic pressure post-graduation. Learning Impact remains similarly weak, indicating that the gap between LLM usage and deep learning gains may persist beyond graduation. This comparison underscores that heavy LLM reliance during education may not automatically produce stronger long-term learning outcomes.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations & Documentation

Modeling Assumptions & Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses are honest and accurately reflect participants' experiences and perceptions (self-reported data).
  • The composite scoring formulas (weighted averages or simple sums per dimension) appropriately capture the underlying constructs.
  • Normalization to a 0–1 scale enables fair comparison between students and alumni without introducing significant distortion.
  • The sample (Lewis University CS students and alumni) is representative enough for internal insights, though not for broad generalization.
  • LLM capabilities and student usage patterns remained relatively stable during the survey period.

Limitations:

  • Small-to-moderate sample size limited to one institution, reducing generalizability.
  • Potential response bias (students/alumni who are more engaged with AI may have been more likely to respond).
  • Recall bias in alumni responses, as they reflected retrospectively on past LLM usage.
  • Cross-sectional design (no longitudinal tracking of the same individuals over time).
  • No statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values) was performed on group differences due to sample constraints.

Model Validation: Outputs were validated through manual cross-checks of raw survey data against computed composites, visual inspection of distributions for anomalies, and consistency checks between radar charts and bar comparisons. All graphs were regenerated from the same processed dataset to ensure reproducibility.

Data Update Logic

To enable dynamic updates when new survey responses are collected:

  1. Add new responses to the master dataset file (e.g., data/survey_responses.csv or data/raw_data.xlsx).
  2. Run the analysis script: python src/analyze_survey.py (this script performs data cleaning, composite score calculation, normalization, and regenerates all figures in the Figures/ and docs/figures/ folders).
  3. Commit and push the updated figures and data files to GitHub — the live website will automatically reflect the new visualizations upon the next deployment.

This logic ensures the website remains current with minimal manual intervention.

Repository Usage

How to use and navigate the repository:

  • Root Structure:
    • /data/ — Raw and processed survey data
    • /src/ — Python analysis scripts (data cleaning, scoring, plotting)
    • /Figures/ and /docs/figures/ — All generated visualizations
    • /pages/ or root — HTML website files (index.html, results.html, etc.)
  • Clone the repo: git clone https://github.com/anthonyjmitchell-byte/LLM-Learning-Consultants.git
  • Install dependencies: pip install -r requirements.txt (includes pandas, matplotlib, plotly, etc.)
  • Run analysis: python src/analyze_survey.py
  • Open results.html locally or deploy via GitHub Pages.

A detailed README.md is available in the repository root with additional setup instructions and folder explanations.

Website Navigation & Interaction

The website is a simple static multi-page site hosted on GitHub Pages. Users interact with it as follows:

  • Home (index.html): Project overview, objectives, and links to Results & Analysis.
  • Results & Analysis (this page): All graphs with detailed interpretations, key findings, and the methodology section above.
  • Navigation: Use the "← Back to Home" button or browser back button. The site is fully responsive and works well on desktop and mobile.
  • Interaction: All content is static but designed for easy reading. Graphs are high-resolution and include alt text for accessibility. No login or dynamic forms are required — users simply read and interpret the visualizations and documentation.

This design prioritizes clarity, reproducibility, and ease of use for instructors, students, and future researchers.
