Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Detailed Analysis of Histograms (Figure 1)

The histograms with kernel density overlays reveal how Computer Science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and Benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating widespread adoption and perceived productivity gains. In contrast, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a critical gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, yet they report limited deep learning outcomes. Dependency is bimodal, suggesting two distinct groups — light and heavy users. Risk perception is moderately spread, while Behavioral Change remains mostly low.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Detailed Analysis and Interpretation of the Boxplot (Figure 2)

This boxplot provides a clear statistical summary of central tendency (median), spread (interquartile range), and outliers for each of the six composite scores. Usage Frequency and Perceived Risks show higher medians, while Perceived Learning Impact has the lowest median and widest variability. This visual strongly confirms the weak translation from heavy LLM usage to genuine deep learning outcomes in Computer Science education.

Meaning and Interpretation (Summary): The boxplot shows central tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, while Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, confirming weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Key Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage (mean ~2.1/3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains (mean ~1.7/3.25)
  • Relatively high Perceived Benefits
  • Moderately high Risk Level
  • Low average Problematic Behavior

Individual Score Distributions

Figure 3: Distribution of LLM Usage Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – Usage Frequency (Mean = 1.97)

The distribution is right-skewed with a clear peak between 1.5 and 2.0, followed by another rise at the highest bin (3.0). The red dashed line marks the mean at 1.97. This indicates strong and frequent adoption of LLMs among Computer Science students for daily academic tasks such as coding, debugging, research, and assignment completion. A significant portion of students uses LLMs at moderate-to-high intensity, confirming that these tools have become a standard part of the modern CS workflow.

Meaning: Strong adoption of LLMs in daily academic tasks.

Figure 4: Distribution of LLM Dependency Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – LLM Dependency (Mean = 2.02)

This distribution shows a prominent peak at 2.0 and a secondary rise at 3.0, with the mean at 2.02. The shape suggests moderate-to-high reliance on LLMs for core academic tasks. While some students maintain lower dependency (scores below 1.5), a large group has integrated LLMs deeply into their problem-solving process. This bimodal tendency points to two distinct user types: those who use LLMs occasionally as a supplement and those who treat them as a primary assistance tool.

Meaning: Moderate-to-high reliance on LLMs for core tasks.

Figure 5: Distribution of Perceived Learning Impact Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – Perceived Learning Impact (Mean = 1.60)

The distribution is centered around 1.6–1.8 with a mean of 1.60. Although a smaller group reports higher learning gains (around 2.0–2.2), the majority of responses cluster at moderate-to-low scores. This pattern reveals a critical gap: despite high usage and dependency, students perceive only limited improvements in deep conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention of Computer Science knowledge. The density curve further emphasizes that true learning gains lag behind adoption rates.

Meaning and Interpretation: Shifted toward lower scores, showing limited perceived gains in conceptual understanding and critical thinking despite high usage.

Figure 6: Distribution of Perceived Benefit Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – Perceived Benefits (Mean = 1.12)

This distribution is heavily right-skewed, with the highest bar at the maximum score (~1.4–1.5) and a mean of 1.12. Students overwhelmingly report strong practical benefits from LLM use, particularly in productivity, speed of task completion, and assistance with routine coding/debugging work. However, the shape suggests these benefits are mostly surface-level rather than transformative for deeper learning.

Meaning: Clear productivity benefits, but mostly surface-level.

Figure 7: Distribution of Perceived Risk Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – Perceived Risks (Mean = 2.48)

The distribution is roughly bell-shaped with a peak around 2.5–3.0 and a mean of 2.48. A notable portion of students also scores at the higher end (4–5). This indicates reasonable to moderately high awareness of the risks associated with LLM use, including potential skill atrophy, over-dependence, ethical concerns, and accuracy issues. The spread shows varied levels of risk perception across the student population.

Meaning: Reasonable awareness of risks associated with LLM use.

Figure 8: Distribution of Problematic Behavior Scores (Students)

Detailed Analysis – Behavioral Change (Mean = 0.45)

This distribution is heavily skewed toward low scores, with a mean of only 0.45. Most students report minimal problematic behavioral changes. However, a visible tail extends to higher scores (0.8–1.0), indicating that a small subset of students may be experiencing more concerning shifts, such as reduced independent problem-solving or over-reliance on AI-generated solutions. Overall, extreme negative behaviors remain limited.

Meaning: Generally low problematic behaviors, though some higher scores exist.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations & Documentation

Modeling Assumptions & Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences (self-reported data).
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs.
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without major distortion.
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights.
  • LLM usage patterns were relatively stable during data collection.

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one institution (reduced generalizability).
  • Possible response and recall bias (especially for alumni).
  • Cross-sectional design (no longitudinal tracking).
  • No statistical significance testing performed due to sample size.

Model Validation: Outputs were cross-checked against raw data, distributions inspected for anomalies, and all graphs regenerated from the same dataset for reproducibility.
