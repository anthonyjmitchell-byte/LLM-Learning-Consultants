Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Analysis of Histograms

The histograms with kernel density overlays reveal how Computer Science students’ experiences with LLMs vary across six dimensions. Usage and Benefit show strong right-skew toward high values, indicating widespread adoption and perceived productivity gains. In contrast, Perceived Learning Impact peaks at lower scores, highlighting a critical gap: students use LLMs frequently and find them beneficial for immediate tasks, yet they report limited deep learning outcomes. Dependency is bimodal, suggesting two distinct groups — light and heavy users. Risk perception is moderately spread, while Behavioral Change remains mostly low.

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Analysis and Interpretation of the Boxplot

This boxplot provides a clear statistical summary of central tendency (median), spread (interquartile range), and outliers for each of the six composite scores. Usage Frequency and Perceived Risks show higher medians, while Perceived Learning Impact has the lowest median and widest variability. This visual strongly confirms the weak translation from heavy LLM usage to genuine deep learning outcomes in Computer Science education.

Meaning and Interpretation: The boxplot shows central tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, while Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, confirming weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage (mean ~2.1/3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains (mean ~1.7/3.25)
  • Relatively high Perceived Benefits
  • Moderately high Risk Level
  • Low average Problematic Behavior

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates. Normalization allows direct visual and statistical comparison across groups despite potential differences in how the original scales were constructed.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Detailed Interpretation

The radar chart for current Computer Science students reveals a distinct profile characterized by prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency. These high scores indicate that today’s undergraduates have deeply integrated Large Language Models into their daily academic routines — from code generation and debugging to idea generation and assignment support.

In contrast, the Learning Impact spoke is noticeably shorter, reflecting limited perceived gains in deep conceptual understanding, critical thinking, and long-term retention of core CS competencies. Moderate scores in Perceived Benefits suggest students clearly value the productivity advantages LLMs provide, while Risk and Behavior remain at low-to-moderate levels.

2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Detailed Interpretation

The alumni radar chart shows a more balanced but still concerning profile. Compared to current students, alumni report noticeably lower Dependency and Usage Frequency. The Learning Impact spoke remains short and weak — similar to that of current students.

3. LLM Impact Comparison: Students vs Alumni

Figure 11: LLM Impact Comparison – Students vs Alumni

Detailed Interpretation

The side-by-side bar comparison shows current students score substantially higher on Usage Frequency and LLM Dependency. Both groups display very low scores on Learning Impact. This exposes a systemic gap between rapid LLM adoption and meaningful improvements in core Computer Science competencies.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations, and Documentation

Modeling Assumptions and Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences
  • Composite scoring formulas capture the constructs
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without major distortion
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights
  • LLM usage patterns were relatively stable during data collection

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one Lewis University which reduces generalizability
  • Possible response and recall bias - especially for alumni
