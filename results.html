Composite Scores Overview

Histograms and boxplots showing the distribution of composite scores across six key dimensions for current Computer Science students.

Figure 1: Histograms of Composite Scores (Dependency, Learning, Behavior, Benefit, Usage, Risk)

Meaning and Interpretation: These histograms illustrate the distribution of student responses across the six LLM impact dimensions. Usage and Risk are skewed toward higher values, while Learning peaks at lower scores. This highlights the gap between frequent LLM adoption and perceived deep learning gains.

Figure 2: Boxplot Comparison of Composite Scores

Meaning and Interpretation: The boxplot shows central tendency and spread. Usage and Risk have higher medians, while Learning has the lowest median and widest variability, confirming weak translation from usage to deep learning outcomes.

Key Findings from Composite Scores:
  • High Usage (mean ~2.1/3.0)
  • Moderate Learning Gains (mean ~1.7/3.25)
  • Relatively high Perceived Benefits
  • Moderately high Risk Level
  • Low average Problematic Behavior

Survey Analysis Results: Students vs Alumni

All composite scores in this section are normalized to a 0–1 scale for fair comparison between current undergraduate students and alumni/graduates.

1. Student LLM Impact Profile

Figure 9: Student LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Meaning and Interpretation: This radar chart shows prominent spikes in Usage and Dependency with a shorter Learning Impact spoke. Current CS students heavily integrate LLMs but see limited gains in deep learning skills. Moderate Benefit and low-to-moderate Risk/Behavior indicate productivity advantages with reasonable caution. This fulfills the project goal of mapping integration patterns and highlights the need for better pedagogical strategies.

2. Alumni LLM Impact Profile

Figure 10: Alumni LLM Impact Profile (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Meaning and Interpretation: Alumni show lower Dependency and Usage than current students. Learning Impact remains weak, suggesting the usage–learning gap may persist after graduation. Benefit and Risk/Behavior levels are comparable. This indicates that heavy LLM reliance during studies does not automatically yield stronger long-term learning outcomes.

3. LLM Impact Comparison: Students vs Alumni

Figure 11: LLM Impact Comparison – Students vs Alumni (Normalized 0–1 Scale)

Meaning and Interpretation: Students score higher on Usage and Dependency. Both groups show modest Benefit and strikingly low Learning Impact. This exposes a systemic gap between LLM adoption and improvements in core CS competencies, strongly supporting the capstone’s central thesis.

Methodology, Assumptions, Limitations & Documentation

Modeling Assumptions & Limitations

Key Assumptions:

  • Survey responses accurately reflect participants’ experiences (self-reported data).
  • Composite scoring formulas appropriately capture the constructs.
  • Normalization to 0–1 scale enables fair comparison without major distortion.
  • The Lewis University sample provides useful internal insights.
  • LLM usage patterns were relatively stable during data collection.

Limitations:

  • Sample limited to one institution (reduced generalizability).
  • Possible response and recall bias (especially for alumni).
  • Cross-sectional design (no longitudinal tracking).
  • No statistical significance testing performed due to sample size.

Model Validation: Outputs were cross-checked against raw data, distributions inspected for anomalies, and all graphs regenerated from the same dataset for reproducibility.

Data Update Logic

To keep results current:

  1. Add new responses to data/survey_responses.csv (or the Excel file).
  2. Run python src/analyze_survey.py — this cleans data, calculates scores, normalizes, and regenerates all figures in docs/figures/.
  3. Commit and push changes to GitHub. The live site updates automatically.
